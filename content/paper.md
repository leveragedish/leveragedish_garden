My fiancée and I are making our own invitation suites for our wedding. We were several weeks and several hundred dollars into the process when we realized that we may have bitten off more than we could chew. These are my notes.

## paper types
There are apparently many different types and attributes to stationary. 

#### text vs. cover paper
#### text paper
- Paper designed to be used for, well text. Namely the insides of books, letters, flyers etc.
- Generally comes in lower weights (e.g. 20-80lb)
- Often used for letterhead, envelopes, and inserts.

#### cover paper
- Thick, rigid, more like [[cardstock]]
- Comes in higher weights (65lb - 130lb cover)
- Holds up well for letterpress, die cuts, embossing.

One thing to note is that the weight means different things depending on if its text vs. cover paper. 80lb text paper will be much thinner than 80lb cover.


## paper attributes
### weight
Weight describes how thick or heavy the paper is. There are three main units that I've seen, and shops online will use one, two or three different ways to describe their paper:
- GSM (grams per square meter) - most accurate and used globally
- lb - pounds
- pt - points, a measure of thickness. 1pt = 0.001 inch

### finishes

|Finish|Description|Best For|
|---|---|---|
|**Smooth**|Sleek surface|Laser/inkjet printing|
|**Linen**|Subtle woven texture|Elegant stationery|
|**Laid**|Ribbed pattern|Traditional feel|
|**Vellum**|Soft, slightly toothy|Invitations, drawing|
|**Glossy**|Shiny coating|Photos, brochures|
|**Matte**|Flat, no sheen|Writing, premium prints|
|**Cotton**|Soft, luxurious|Letterpress, formal invites|

### common paper weights
- Copy paper is around 75-90gsm, or 20-24lb text.
- Light cardstock is around 176-216gsm, or 65-70lb **cover**.
- Medium cardstock is 250-300gsm, or 90-110lb cover. These are typically used for invitations and postcards.
- Heavy cardstock is from 300-400gsm, or 120-140lb cover. Think business cards, and luxury invites.
