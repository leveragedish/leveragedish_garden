# Part 1: Principles
## Chapter 1: The Transaction
> Good writing has an aliveness that keeps the reader reading from one paragraph to the next, and it’s not a question of gimmicks to “personalize” the author. It’s a question of using the English language in a way that will achieve the greatest clarity and strength.

Chapter 1 is short. It’s only 3 pages, and says that there are many ways to write, you should find the way that works for you. The goal is to write something interesting. 

## Chapter 2: Simplicity
>The secret to good writing is to strip every sentence to its cleanest components.

Remove anything unnecessary. Clear your mind of clutter. A clear mind leads to clear thinking. Clear thinking leads to clear writing, and thinking clearly is a conscious act that you have to force upon yourself.

## Chapter 3: Clutter
> Writing improves in a direct ratio to the number of things we can keep out of it that shouldn’t be there.

Work to clean up your sentences. Say what you want to be said and don’t beat around the bush. “Beware then, of the long word that’s no better than the short word.”

He points out another bad habit, when people say “It is interesting to note”. Don’t just say something is interesting. If it is interesting, then make it interesting with your words.
Revision is writing, you can often cut out up to fifty percent of unnecessary words from your first draft.

## Chapter 4: Style
> There is no style store; style is organic to the person doing the writing.

You will reveal your own style through the act of writing. Don’t hold your writing with such high expectations that it is difficult to begin. Just start. If you try to write too fancifully, then the reader will notice and it will feel like you’re a bald man wearing a wig. Be yourself.

## Chapter 5: The Audience
Who are you writing for? Yourself. Don’t try to be who you aren’t when you’re writing. If you wouldn’t use a certain phrase or wording in casual conversation, don’t use it in your writing. Be authentic to yourself. 

>You are writing primarily to please yourself, and if you go about it with enjoyment you will also entertain the readers who are worth writing for. If you lose the dullards back in the dust, you don’t want them anyway.

## Chapter 6: Words
>You’ll never make your mark as a writer unless you develop a respect for words and a curiosity about their shades of meaning that is almost obsessive. 

If you want to be a great writer, you have to truly care about words. Carry a thesaurus, if you don’t know what a word means, look it up. The language is always changing and you should be reading voraciously. Don’t use cliches, that’s being lazy. Read your writing out loud, it should sound good to the ear.

## Chapter 7: Usage
>Language is a fabric that changes from one week to another, adding new strands and dropping old ones, and even word freaks fight over what is allowable, often reaching their decision on a wholly subjective basis such as taste. Which still leaves the question of who our tastemakers are.

Given that the language is constantly evolving and new words and jargon are being added every day, how should you decide what words are usable and what are not? He talks about how he is on a national writers panel that is tasked with filling a survey on what new words gain approval. There is frequently disagreement among the panel, and some members are stuffier than others. He suggests being more liberal in word usage and conservative in grammar. Try to use words that are actively making the language better.

# Part II: Methods
## Chapter 8: Unity
Be consistent in both your tone and perspective. Decide on what you want to write and how. If you flip back and forth between serious and casual, or first and third person, or past and present tense, it can be very jarring and throw off the reader.

## Chapter 9: The Lead and The Ending
Think very carefully about your first sentence, and just as carefully about your last. Your first sentence should hook people and make them want to read the second, and the third, and so on. The sentences and paragraphs should build. Focus on the one point you want to get across. Don’t let your writing drag on too long. Writing that starts out tight and controlled can be ruined by rambling and keeping on when it should have ended. Finish when you are done telling what you wanted to say. 

## Chapter 10: Bits and Pieces
Use active verbs when possible. Most adjectives and adverbs are unnecessary, there are plenty of words that can get across both the meaning and the feeling you want to convey. The biggest lesson from this chapter for me was the bashing of using “little qualifiers”. Zinsser is telling us to “say it with your chest”, and that using things like “pretty”, “very”, “quite”, “too” just dilute your writing and also make the reader less confident in what you’re saying. I have to admit, I’m a big offender in this way, and I’ll be more conscious of my usage of these “little qualifiers” moving forward. 

>”Don’t hedge your prose with little timidities. Good writing is leas and confident.”

>”Every little qualifier whittles away some fraction of the reader’s trust.”

He also advises to not use exclamation points unless you must. Exclamation points are cringe. Instead of using them, make your sentence in such a way that the words will put the emphasis where you want it. I’m also guilty of this sin, I feel like Zinsser is talking directly to me. I often reach for the exclamation point as if I’m texting someone, I think I probably overuse them in those instances as well. 

This chapter is split into tiny sections each going over specific instances of parts of writing like when to use contractions, commas, emdashes, and concept nouns. I’m only including the parts that I found most interesting. His section on Concept Nouns brings up an important insight: the eerie-ness of sentences that don’t have people in them. Get people doing things. 

On overstatement. Don’t. If you’re constantly overstating events for the drama, you will lose credibility. 

He also has a large segment on rewriting.

>Rewriting is the essence of writing well: it’s where the game is won or lost. 

>Writing is like a good watch — it should run smoothly and have no extra parts.

>Keep putting yourself in the reader’s place. Is there something he should have been told early in the sentence that you put near the end?

Read your writing out loud. When you do, you’ll hear the gaps and places where you lost or confused the reader. You have to make your writing hold together from start to finish and move efficiently.

# Part 3: Forms
## Chapter 11: Nonfiction as Literature
Fiction is overrated. For most people learning to write, they should write about what they know. The real world and their experience is a lot easier to write about than something they need to invent.

## Chapter 12: Writing About People
Using quotes from real people can help get your message across better than you telling the reader. Go out and interview people. Take notes. Make sure to represent your interviewee’s position accurately. 
> When you use a quotation, start the sentence with it. Don’t lead up to it with a vapid phrase saying what the man said.

## Chapter 13: Writing About Places
This is the chapter on travel writing, which given how much my fiancée and I travel this is especially pertinent. People don’t want to hear all the details about your trip. They only want to hear some. What can you tell them that they don’t already know, especially if you went to a common tourist place like the Eiffel Tower. When writing about your travels, choose your words with unusual care, find interesting and significant details, and get rid of everything that’s already a known attribute. The whole point is to find the central idea of the place you’re dealing with. Try to draw the best out of it, and say something interesting. 

## Chapter 14: Writing About Yourself
>Memoir isn’t the summary of a life; its a window into a life, very much like a photograph in its selective composition.

>If you write for yourself, you’ll reach the people you want to write for.

At its core, the memoir is a work of introspection. Zinsser loves reading memoirs because of how they give us a window into the author’s soul. Inherently, it’s deeply personal, and everyone has their own story to tell. Some people may be self conscious about writing a memoir, but there is a difference between having an ego and being egotistical. Writing itself is an ego-centric activity, and that’s ok. “Giver yourself permission to write about yourself, and have a good time doing it.”

## Chapter 15: Science and Technology
Scientists often reject writing, and writers often reject science. This probably stems from being told in your youth that you were either a math/science person or a creative person. This notion is a false dichotomy. In fact, writing well about science is essential to give your ideas mass appeal. Write like a person. Use sequential writing. Introduce the reader to something they’re familiar with, and work your way up in complexity. But keep it relatable, and speak plainly. Even in explaining scientific concepts, make your writing have people in it in some way.
